Victory for Fair Pay: Parliament Passes the Digital Workers Rights Act

Canberra · March 3, 2033

In a historic win for the Australian Digital Gig Workers Union (ADGWU), the Digital Workers Rights Act 2033 has officially passed both houses of Parliament, ushering in a new era of protections for digital and platform workers. The change comes in an industry long plagued by algorithmic management and precarity, according to Stewart & Crawford (2022, p.12).

The Act mandates a series of baseline protections, including:

  • Minimum hourly pay guarantees
  • Superannuation inclusion
  • Collective bargaining rights
  • Transparency on ratings, fees, and algorithmic decisions
  • Mandated rest periods
  • Access to dispute resolution
  • Health and safety protections
  • Limits on unpaid waiting time

Crucially, it defines “digital workers” as individuals performing labour mediated by algorithmic systems, including freelancers, content creators, moderators, designers, transcriptionists, and platform-based service providers.

ADGWU President Anna Liu described the reform as “the most significant workplace law since the Fair Work Act 2009.” She added:

“For too long, digital labour has been disguised as ‘entrepreneurship’. This Act makes clear that control through algorithms is still control — and that means rights.”

Global organisations such as Uber, UStream and Deliveroo criticised the legislation, claiming compliance will “reduce platform agility.” But labour researchers argue the reform is overdue. As Kyloh (2018, p.235) noted, Australian workers face some of the world’s highest levels of non-standard work, reporting little to no job security due to weak regulation.

For ADGWU members, however, the passage of the Act marks the beginning of a more secure digital future.
