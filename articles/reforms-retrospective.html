Reforms Have Arrived: A Look Back in Time

Sydney · January 18, 2034

From early guardrails for drivers and delivery workers to a final push on platform transparency, this retrospective tracks how advocacy, inquiries, and organising shaped today’s protections.

In 2024, the federal government introduced first-step protections for rideshare and delivery workers, recognising that gig labour required a safety net (Minister for Employment 2024). Yet as research highlighted, loopholes remained — particularly around unpaid waiting time and automated pay changes. Scholars noted the precarious nature of digital labour, with algorithmic control reducing worker autonomy (Woodcock & Graham 2019; Prassl 2018).

Subsequent inquiries and reports, including the Victorian Government’s Inquiry into the On-Demand Workforce (2020), identified widespread issues of insecurity, low pay, and lack of dispute resolution pathways. Cases before the Fair Work Commission, such as MS2024-3, further demonstrated the need for minimum standards to cover regulated workers.

By 2030 it was clear these piecemeal reforms were not enough. Building on the work of TWU and AUWU, ADGWU campaigned to close remaining gaps and standardise rights across platforms. As one analysis observed, Australian workers were among the most exposed to precarity globally (Kyloh 2018).

These efforts culminated in the passage of the Digital Workers Rights Act 2033, the landmark legislation that finally set baseline protections for all digital workers.
