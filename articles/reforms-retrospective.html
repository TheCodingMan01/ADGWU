Reforms Have Arrived: A Look Back in Time

Sydney · January 18, 2034

As digital workers bask in the glory of the Digital Workers Rights Act, championed by the ADGWU, we take a moment to reflect back on the early stages of gig work, and the trials and tribulations experienced by those pioneering the industry.

In 2024, the federal government announced long-awaited workplace protections for rideshare, food delivery and truck drivers, including ensuring platforms must follow a fair process when deactivating workers, but in 2030, critics say the reforms hadn’t kept pace with the growing dominance of gig platforms.

These measures followed pressure from organisations like the Transport Workers Union (TWU) and the Australian Unemployed Workers’ Union, who campaigned throughout the 2020s to close legal loopholes that allowed platforms to avoid treating workers as employees.

Other issues were also prevalent particularly to marginalised workers, for example women working dual roles as platform workers and also caregivers. Platform work was also shown to further reinforce existing inequalities rather than mitigate them, as told in a report by Flanagan. Further to this, platform owners maintained that they were simply intermediaries between the gig workers and the work, and were not responsible for ensuring workers had minimum wage or other benefits.

The Fair Work Commission began reviewing applications for ‘employee like’ benefits submitted by the TWU in 2025, which had responses from internal platform giants such as Uber, Amazon, DoorDash and MenuLog.

Victoria’s Inquiry into the On-Demand Workforce (2020) had already recommended enforceable minimum standards and a licensing scheme for platforms. Yet, many of those recommendations remain unfulfilled.

We are now pleased to announce that ADGWU have successfully passed legislation that provides Australian gig workers with the following benefits previously only experienced by full time and part time staff:

  • Guaranteed minimum pay
  • Superannuation inclusion
  • Access to dispute resolution
  • Health and safety protections
  • Limits on unpaid waiting time
